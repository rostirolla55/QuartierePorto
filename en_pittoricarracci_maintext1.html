Via Riva di Reno in Bologna covers the underground route of the Reno Canal .

Here are some important details:

  • The Reno Canal: One of Bologna's main man-made canals, it originates from the Reno River near the Casalecchio Lock. This canal has been vital to the city's economy since the Middle Ages, powering mills, silk mills, and other industrial activities.

  • The Covering: The canal in that section (and in much of the historic center) was progressively covered, or "tombato", starting from the mid-twentieth century for traffic and urban redevelopment needs, thus creating space for the current road.

  • The Name: The name of the street itself ("Riva di Reno") recalls its original function as the bank or shore of the canal.

  • Current Curiosity: In recent years, during the works for the new tram line, the rediscovery of some short stretches of the Reno Canal has been discussed and is in part being realised

The road covers the route of what was once an important open-air waterway.

From the 14th-century walls surrounding the city , at Via Sabotino, we find the Grada Church, which covers the Reno Canal. The grate that once served to stop floating objects entering the city from the canal can still be seen today. The bodies of people who drowned in the canal have also been found.

The Carracci family, active in Bologna between the late 16th and early 17th centuries, are crucial figures in the history of Italian art, as they marked the transition from Mannerism to Baroque and founded the famous Bolognese school of painting.

The most innovative contribution of the Carraccis was not only their painting, but the foundation, around 1582 , of an artistic workshop that later became known as the Accademia degli Incamminati (or Accademia del Naturale or dei Desiderosi ).

  • Purpose: To overcome the formal excesses and artificiality of Mannerism.

  • Teaching Method: The Academy introduced a modern teaching method based on:

    • Life study ( drawing of nude models and anatomy).

    • Copying the great masters of the Renaissance (such as Raphael, Titian and Correggio).

    • Discussions of art theory, literature, and poetry.

  • Legacy: They trained the major artists of the seventeenth-century Bolognese school, including Guido Reni and Domenichino .

üñºÔ∏è The Artistic Reform and Style

The Carraccis advocated a "return to nature" and a clearer, more emotional and accessible painting , anticipating the principles of Counter-Reformation art.

  • Historical and Religious Painting: Their religious and mythological works are characterized by a strong naturalism and a composition that combines the balance of the classical tradition (Raphael) with the colorism and sensitivity of Venetian light (Titian and Correggio).

  • Frescoes of Palazzo Farnese (Rome): Annibale Carracci's masterpiece (with the help of Agostino) was the decoration of the Galleria Farnese in Rome (1597-1608), which became the model for Roman Baroque painting and the mythological fresco of the following centuries.

  • Genre Painting: Annibale Carracci was also a pioneer in genre painting , elevating scenes of everyday life (such as The Bean Eater or the two versions of The Butcher's Shop ) to subjects of great artistic dignity through a careful realism free from excessive idealization.



The famous work known as "The Butcher's Shop" was painted by Annibale Carracci (1560‚Äì1609).

It is one of his most important genre paintings , that is, scenes depicting moments of everyday life. This work in particular is remarkable because, despite depicting a humble subject, it does so with great dignity, realism, and a complex, monumental composition.

Annibale is cited as an artist who also excelled in genre painting, citing The Bean Eater as an example of this realism. "The Butcher's Shop" is another splendid example of this sensibility.



It is a bit of a mystery, since the circumstances of its construction, dated around 1585 , are not known with certainty .

There are several hypotheses about the clients:

  • Canobi Family : One hypothesis is that the work was intended for a well-known Bolognese family of wealthy meat traders, the Canobi , owners of a large butcher's shop in the city. However, archival research has not provided definitive proof.

  • Butchers' Guild: Another theory is that the painting may have been commissioned by the butchers' guild ("beccai") to decorate its headquarters.

What is certain is that the painting, also known as the Great Butcher's Shop , passed from the Gonzaga family collection to the King of England Charles I Stuart in 1627 .
