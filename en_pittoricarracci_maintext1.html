Via Riva di Reno in Bologna covers the underground route of the Reno Canal .

Here are some important details:

  • The Reno Canal: One of Bologna's main man-made canals, it originates from the Reno River near the Casalecchio Lock. This canal has been vital to the city's economy since the Middle Ages, powering mills, silk mills, and other industrial activities.

  • The Covering: The canal in that section (and in much of the historic center) was progressively covered, or "tombato", starting from the mid-twentieth century for traffic and urban redevelopment needs, thus creating space for the current road.

  • The Name: The name of the street itself ("Riva di Reno") recalls its original function as the bank or shore of the canal.

  • Current Curiosity: In recent years, during the works for the new tram line, the rediscovery of some short stretches of the Reno Canal has been discussed and is in part being realised

The most innovative contribution of the Carraccis was not only their painting, but the foundation, around 1582 , of an artistic workshop that later became known as the Accademia degli Incamminati (or Accademia del Naturale or dei Desiderosi ).

  • Purpose: To overcome the formal excesses and artificiality of Mannerism.

  • Teaching Method: The Academy introduced a modern teaching method based on:

    • Life study ( drawing of nude models and anatomy).

    • Copying the great masters of the Renaissance (such as Raphael, Titian and Correggio).

    • Discussions of art theory, literature, and poetry.

  • Legacy: They trained the major artists of the seventeenth-century Bolognese school, including Guido Reni and Domenichino .

üñºÔ∏è The Artistic Reform and Style

The Carraccis advocated a "return to nature" and a clearer, more emotional and accessible painting , anticipating the principles of Counter-Reformation art.

  • Historical and Religious Painting: Their religious and mythological works are characterized by a strong naturalism and a composition that combines the balance of the classical tradition (Raphael) with the colorism and sensitivity of Venetian light (Titian and Correggio).

  • Frescoes of Palazzo Farnese (Rome): Annibale Carracci's masterpiece (with the help of Agostino) was the decoration of the Galleria Farnese in Rome (1597-1608), which became the model for Roman Baroque painting and the mythological fresco of the following centuries.

  • Genre Painting: Annibale Carracci was also a pioneer in genre painting , elevating scenes of everyday life (such as The Bean Eater or the two versions of The Butcher's Shop ) to subjects of great artistic dignity through a careful realism free from excessive idealization.

The Carracci: The Fathers of the Baroque in Bologna

The Carraccis were a family of Bolognese artists composed of three central figures: Ludovico Carracci (1555‚Äì1619), his cousin Agostino Carracci (1557‚Äì1602), and Agostino's brother, Annibale Carracci (1560‚Äì1609).

Their work and artistic philosophy are considered the foundation of Baroque painting, as they proposed a stylistic "reform" that overcame the artificiality of Mannerism to return to a vigorous naturalism and a revival of the great classical models of the Renaissance (such as Raphael and Titian).

1. The Foundation of the Academy of the Incamminati

The Carraccis' most revolutionary contribution was the foundation in Bologna, around 1582, of a private school that took the name of Accademia dei Desiderosi , later renamed Accademia degli Incamminati (that is, "those who are on the right path of art").

This academy is considered the first modern art institution in Italy for several reasons:

  • Study of Life: He encouraged the direct study of nature and the human body (the "Life Drawing"), breaking with the Mannerist practice of copying only the models of previous masters.

  • Complete Education : In addition to drawing and painting, the Academy offered lessons in anatomy, geometry, perspective, and literature, elevating the artist from a mere craftsman to a complete intellectual.

  • Stylistic Summary : The Carraccis taught how to combine the rigorous drawing of the Tuscan-Roman tradition (Michelangelo, Raphael) with the colour and use of light typical of the Venetian school (Titian, Correggio).

2. The Main Works

The three Carraccis worked closely together for many years, often making it difficult to distinguish individual hands in the early fresco cycles in Bologna:

Bologna Period (Collaboration)

  • Palazzo Fava, Bologna (1584): Frescoes with the Stories of Jason and Medea and the Stories of Aeneas .

  • Palazzo Magnani, Bologna (1589-1592): The famous frieze depicting the Foundation of Rome , a masterpiece of narrative clarity.

Maturity in Rome (Hannibal)

In 1595, Annibale and Agostino were summoned to Rome by Cardinal Odoardo Farnese. Annibale in particular produced the work that would become the manifesto of Baroque classicism:

  • Farnese Gallery , Rome (1597-1601): The frescoes depicting The Loves of the Gods . This cycle is considered a masterpiece of monumental fresco painting and was a point of reference for entire generations of subsequent artists, including Lanfranco and Pietro da Cortona.

3. The Three Artists

  • Annibale Carracci (1560-1609): The most gifted and innovative of the trio. He excelled in both large-scale mythological frescoes (as in the Palazzo Farnese) and genre painting (such as The Bean Eater ), displaying a realism and sensitivity to everyday life that anticipated the work of Caravaggio. He is buried in the Pantheon next to Raphael.

  • Ludovico Carracci (1555-1619): The eldest and the intellectual and theoretical leader of the group. He remained mainly in Bologna, focusing primarily on religious works of the Counter-Reformation, characterized by profound devotion and emotional intensity.

  • Agostino Carracci (1557-1602): A versatile artist, also known as an engraver and theorist. He collaborated actively with his brother and cousin, contributing significantly to the decorations of Bologna and Rome.

ü•© Meaning of "Beccaro"

"Beccaro" is a term of ancient dialectal origin (especially Lombard and Northern Italian ) which means:

  • Butcher (the most common meaning).

  • Butcher (synonym of butcher, although it sometimes indicated the breeder of cattle destined for slaughter).

In short, it indicated the person who slaughtered animals or sold meat for a living.

üåé Origin and Etymology

The origin of the word " beccaro " is of a professional nature and is linked to the trade described above.

  • It derives from the word "becco" which, in Latin ( beccus ), can mean "beak" (of the bird), but in the butcher's context refers to the male goat or, more generally, to cattle.

  • One of the most popular hypotheses is that the term is linked to the meat of goat (beak) or farmyard animals, which was very popular in the Middle Ages.

  • The form " beccaro " is considered the ancestor or dialectal form (ancient or regional) of the modern Italian "beccaio" .

This term, like many medieval trade names, is at the origin of numerous Italian surnames such as Beccari , Beccaro and the illustrious Beccaria (for example, Cesare Beccaria, the author of Dei delitti e delle pene ).
